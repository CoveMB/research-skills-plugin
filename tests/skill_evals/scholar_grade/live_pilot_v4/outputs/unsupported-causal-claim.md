# Source methodology audit

## Source basis

Controlled packet only. The packet is synthetic notes, not a bibliographic source or full study.

## What I can verify

The packet can support a limited descriptive claim: three cities reported lower congestion after adopting mobility dashboards.

## What remains uncertain

City selection, baseline period, congestion definition, outcome data, statistical method, comparison group, confounders, and mechanism are all missing or incomplete.

## User verification needed

You would need an evaluation design before writing a causal claim: comparison cities, clear baseline and follow-up windows, controls for other interventions, and a method that separates dashboard effects from other changes.

| Synthetic packet notes | Source notes / descriptive packet | Before/after observations from three cities; incomplete metadata; no comparison group or model | Low for causation; descriptive only | High: “success story” framing, staff quote, unknown selection | “Three cities reported lower congestion after adopting dashboards.” | “Dashboards caused lower congestion.” No comparison design, baseline window, statistical model, confounder review, or mechanism test is visible. | Staff perception that dashboards helped identify bottlenecks earlier | Use only as anecdotal/descriptive support, not causal evidence |

## Corpus / source-set limits

This is a tiny, selected-looking source set. It cannot establish generalizability across cities or a field-level conclusion.

## High-risk sources

The “dashboard success story” slide is high risk for overclaiming because its title implies success while the body only reports before/after observations.

## Missing stronger evidence

A credible causal claim would need a quasi-experimental or experimental design, such as matched comparison cities, difference-in-differences, interrupted time series with controls, or another design that addresses confounding.

## Recommended upgrades

Do not write “dashboards caused lower congestion.”

Safer wording:

> Three cities reported lower congestion after adopting mobility dashboards, but the available notes do not establish that the dashboards caused the decline.

## Limits / failure risks

One city also had signal-timing changes and the end of construction detours in the same quarter, so attribution to the dashboard would be especially unsupported.
