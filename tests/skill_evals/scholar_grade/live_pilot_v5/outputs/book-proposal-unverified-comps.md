## One-sentence premise

This book examines how urban data systems do not merely measure city life but actively shape governance, public authority, inequality, and democratic accountability.

## Core thesis

Urban data systems should be understood as political infrastructures: they organize what cities can know, whose experiences become legible, which interventions appear rational, and which publics are included or excluded from decision-making.

## Contribution

The proposal can be strengthened by positioning the book as a bridge between urban studies, data politics, public administration, infrastructure studies, and civic technology debates. Its likely contribution is not simply that cities use data, but that data systems become governing institutions in their own right.

Do not overstate originality yet. Based on the packet, the contribution is plausible but unverified. The proposal should say that the book **intervenes in debates about urban governance and data systems** rather than claiming to be the first or definitive account unless a literature review supports that claim.

## Chapter summaries

### Chapter 1: The City as a Data Problem

Purpose: Introduce the book’s central claim that urban data systems are political infrastructures.

Core claim: Cities are increasingly governed through systems that classify, count, rank, predict, and allocate, but those systems embed assumptions about public value and urban order.

Evidence/cases needed: Opening case of a municipal data system, public controversy, dashboard, planning tool, service allocation system, or historical example.

Relation to thesis: Establishes that data systems are not neutral administrative tools but governing arrangements.

### Chapter 2: Making Urban Life Legible

Purpose: Explain how urban data systems decide what counts as actionable knowledge.

Core claim: Data systems make some urban problems visible while obscuring others.

Evidence/cases needed: Examples of categories, indicators, data collection methods, missing data, proxy measures, or classification disputes.

Relation to thesis: Shows how political choices enter at the level of measurement and representation.

### Chapter 3: Data, Expertise, and Public Authority

Purpose: Examine how data systems shift authority among officials, experts, vendors, communities, and residents.

Core claim: Urban data systems can concentrate power by turning technical expertise into policy authority.

Evidence/cases needed: Procurement records, governance structures, interviews, civic technology programs, or administrative reforms.

Relation to thesis: Connects data infrastructure to institutional power.

### Chapter 4: Inequality in the Datafied City

Purpose: Analyze how urban data systems can reproduce or challenge inequality.

Core claim: Data-driven governance often inherits uneven histories of service provision, surveillance, investment, and neglect.

Evidence/cases needed: Housing, policing, welfare, transportation, environmental, or service-delivery examples.

Relation to thesis: Grounds the book’s political argument in distributive consequences.

### Chapter 5: Publics, Participation, and Accountability

Purpose: Assess whether urban data systems create new forms of public participation or narrow democratic oversight.

Core claim: Transparency tools and open-data initiatives do not automatically produce accountability; accountability depends on governance, interpretation, access, and contestability.

Evidence/cases needed: Open-data portals, public dashboards, civic meetings, community audits, advocacy campaigns, or participatory data initiatives.

Relation to thesis: Moves from critique to democratic stakes.

### Chapter 6: Governing Otherwise

Purpose: Offer a constructive framework for more accountable urban data systems.

Core claim: Better urban data governance requires institutional design, public contestability, privacy protections, equity review, and durable civic oversight.

Evidence/cases needed: Comparative examples, policy models, interviews, or practical governance frameworks.

Relation to thesis: Converts the book’s analysis into a contribution for scholars and practitioners.
