## Charitable restatement

In its strongest form, the thesis argues that urban planning dashboards can make planning processes more democratic by making information more visible, legible, and accountable to residents who would otherwise lack access to technical planning data. If dashboards expose decisions, metrics, timelines, tradeoffs, and public-service outcomes, they may lower barriers to civic oversight and give more people a practical basis for participation.

## Rival explanations

Dashboards may correlate with democratization because democratic institutions are already more likely to adopt transparent tools. They may improve administrative efficiency rather than public power. They may serve symbolic legitimacy, allowing governments to appear accountable while avoiding deeper participation. They may benefit intermediaries such as journalists, NGOs, consultants, or advocacy groups more than ordinary residents. They may also centralize authority by defining which metrics count.

## Missing literatures or perspectives

Likely areas to check include planning theory, participatory governance, critical data studies, civic technology, urban informatics, public administration, accessibility studies, digital divide research, surveillance studies, and political theory on transparency and accountability.

Because the corpus is one-sided, these are not proven gaps in the literature; they are necessary checks before claiming the thesis is well supported.

## Specialist review needed

A planning scholar should review whether “urban planning” is defined too broadly. A methods reviewer should assess whether the source search can support causal claims. An equity or accessibility reviewer should assess whether dashboard access translates into meaningful participation across communities. A privacy or data-governance reviewer should examine risks around public metrics and surveillance.

## Hidden assumptions / bias risks

The thesis risks conflating information access with democratic power. It assumes dashboards are understandable, accessible, trusted, and actionable. It may overgeneralize from successful or promotional cases. It risks selection bias because the search terms invite supportive results. It may also assume that quantified public data is more democratic than qualitative, local, or experiential knowledge.

## Claims to narrow

“Dashboards democratize urban planning” is too strong for the available packet. Narrow to claims such as:

“Dashboards can support democratic accountability in urban planning when they make relevant information accessible, when residents have channels to act on that information, and when dashboard metrics are governed transparently and equitably.”

## Revised stronger thesis

Urban planning dashboards do not automatically democratize planning, but they can become democratic infrastructure under specific conditions: accessible design, public contestability of metrics, institutional responsiveness, privacy safeguards, and clear pathways from public information to public influence. Without those conditions, dashboards may produce transparency without power, or even reinforce technocratic and unequal forms of planning.

## Limits / failure risks

The current packet is not enough to support the preferred thesis. It can justify a preliminary, conditional argument, but not a strong conclusion. The thesis would be materially weakened if broader searching finds that dashboards mainly improve visibility without changing participation, influence, or accountability outcomes.
