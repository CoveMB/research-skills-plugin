## Inclusion criteria

Include sources that meet at least one of these criteria:

- Directly analyze public, municipal, civic, or urban dashboards.
- Analyze open data portals or public performance reporting where dashboards are part of the accountability mechanism.
- Address urban accountability, transparency, responsiveness, oversight, participation, or auditability.
- Provide case evidence from city governments, metropolitan agencies, or urban public-service systems.
- Offer relevant theoretical framing for accountability, transparency, smart cities, audit culture, datafication, or public-sector performance management.
- Provide primary-source evidence about dashboard design, implementation, governance, use, maintenance, or public claims.

Quality and status rules:

- Prefer peer-reviewed articles, academic books, and reputable institutional reports for analytic claims.
- Use city materials and advocacy materials as primary or perspective sources, not neutral proof of impact.
- Track corrections, retractions, questionable journals, predatory outlets, and duplicate versions if discovered during live searching.
- Keep non-scholarly sources when they are objects of analysis, such as municipal claims or civic-tech advocacy.

## Exclusion criteria

Exclude or separate sources that:

- Discuss private business dashboards with no public-sector or urban relevance.
- Discuss smart cities only at a promotional level without accountability, governance, or dashboard relevance.
- Treat dashboards purely as software artifacts with no connection to public decision-making, accountability, or urban governance.
- Are duplicates of the same report, article, or preprint unless version comparison matters.
- Are inaccessible beyond title/snippet when no abstract, full text, or reliable metadata can be checked.
- Make unsupported claims from low-credibility venues unless used as examples of rhetoric or stakeholder positioning.
- Fall outside the confirmed date, geography, or language scope once those boundaries are set.

## Citation-chaining plan

Backward chaining:

- For each foundational article or book found later, inspect its references for accountability theory, transparency studies, public administration, urban governance, smart-city critique, and dashboard-specific studies.

Forward chaining:

- Use citation indexes to identify later works citing each seed source.
- Prioritize works that apply, contest, update, or empirically test the source’s claims.

Lateral chaining:

- Track recurring authors, coauthors, journals, conferences, city cases, policy programs, and keywords.
- Search special issues, edited volumes, and conference tracks connected to digital government, urban informatics, HCI, planning, and public administration.

Disconfirming chaining:

- For pro-dashboard sources, inspect citing works that criticize transparency, public metrics, or smart-city governance.
- For critical sources, inspect citing works that provide evidence of dashboard usefulness, public oversight, or administrative improvement.

## Limits / failure risks

- A planned search cannot establish the state of the literature.
- English-only searching would likely skew results toward Global North scholarship and English-language municipal cases.
- Searching only “smart city” may miss public administration and performance-management literature.
- Searching only “accountability” may miss related concepts such as transparency, oversight, responsiveness, auditability, legitimacy, and participation.
- Searching only academic databases may miss municipal primary sources and grey literature where dashboard claims are made.
- Searching only city websites may overrepresent official narratives.
- Famous-author chaining should be delayed until seed sources exist, otherwise the corpus may become anchored around known theorists rather than the actual dashboard literature.
