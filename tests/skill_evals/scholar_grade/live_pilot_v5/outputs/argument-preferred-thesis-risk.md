# Argument architecture

## Source basis

Source access level: `controlled-packet`.

This architecture uses only the visible packet. The packet gives a preferred thesis, one warrant, one thin evidence note, and three named missing materials. It does not provide source summaries, cases, participation records, citations, opposition evidence, or a literature map.

## What I can verify

The preferred thesis is: “Public dashboards make planning more democratic.”

The visible warrant is: transparency improves participation.

The visible evidence is only that dashboard metrics were published online.

The packet explicitly identifies missing evidence of public participation, a rival explanation that dashboards may reflect administrative capacity rather than democratic change, and missing opposing case material.

## What remains uncertain

It remains uncertain whether the dashboards were used by the public, whether participation increased, whether participation became more inclusive, whether public input affected planning decisions, and whether dashboard publication caused any democratic change.

It also remains uncertain whether dashboard use is better explained by institutional capacity, technical modernization, compliance, performance management, or administrative communication rather than democratization.

## User verification needed

You would need to verify participation records, public engagement data, decision influence, user demographics, case comparisons, administrative capacity indicators, and opposing cases where dashboards did not democratize planning.

## Main thesis

Public dashboards may make planning more democratic when online transparency produces meaningful public participation and influence over planning decisions; however, the current evidence only supports the narrower claim that metrics were made publicly visible, not that planning became more democratic.

## Thesis variants

**Bold thesis:** Public dashboards democratize planning by turning administrative metrics into public instruments for participation, scrutiny, and influence.

**Moderate thesis:** Public dashboards can support more democratic planning when transparency is paired with evidence of public uptake and decision influence.

**Conservative thesis:** Public dashboards increase public visibility into planning metrics, but their democratic effects remain unproven without participation and influence evidence.

**Public-facing thesis:** Dashboards can help the public see how planning works, but visibility alone does not prove that people gained more say.

**Academic thesis:** The democratic significance of public dashboards depends on whether transparency generates participatory capacity, not merely administrative disclosure.

## Hidden assumptions

**Empirical assumptions:** Dashboard users existed; users understood the metrics; participation changed after publication; dashboard-informed participation affected planning decisions.

**Conceptual assumptions:** More transparency can count as democratic improvement only if connected to participation or accountability; democracy in planning means more than information disclosure.

**Normative assumptions:** Public participation is desirable in planning; broader access to planning information is democratically valuable.

**Positional assumptions:** The argument appears sympathetic to dashboard-enabled governance and must guard against treating administrative visibility as democratic reform.

**Methodological assumptions:** A single note about online publication is insufficient for causal or democratic claims; the argument needs process evidence, counter-cases, and rival-explanation testing.

## Chapter-level argument sequence

1. **Define the problem:** Explain why planning democracy cannot be inferred from administrative transparency alone.
2. **Introduce theory:** Clarify the proposed mechanism from dashboard transparency to public participation to democratic influence.
3. **Establish context:** Show what dashboards are, what metrics were published, and who could access them.
4. **Present evidence:** Test whether publication led to public participation.
5. **Test influence:** Examine whether participation shaped planning priorities, decisions, or accountability.
6. **Handle counterargument:** Assess the rival explanation that dashboards reflect administrative capacity rather than democratic change.
7. **Use opposing cases:** Identify where dashboards failed to democratize planning and refine the scope conditions.
8. **Synthesize:** State when dashboards can support democratic planning and when they are only administrative disclosure.

## Weak links

The weakest warrant is “transparency improves participation.” The packet gives only evidence of publication, not evidence of use, participation, inclusion, deliberation, accountability, or influence.

The second weak link is causality. Even if participation increased, the argument still needs evidence that dashboards contributed to that change.

The third weak link is democratic meaning. More available information is not automatically more democratic unless the book defines and demonstrates a democratic mechanism.

## Corpus-dependent weak links

The claim that dashboards generally make planning more democratic is corpus-dependent and currently unverified. The visible packet does not establish case representativeness, literature coverage, field consensus, novelty, or counter-literature balance.

The missing opposing case material creates a one-sided source-set risk. Planned evidence paths do not establish corpus coverage.

## Stronger formulation

Public dashboards should be treated as potential democratic infrastructure, not democratic reform by themselves: they make planning more democratic only when published metrics are accessible, used by publics, and connected to participation that can influence planning decisions.

## Limits / failure risks

The preferred thesis currently risks overstating what the evidence can bear. With only proof that metrics were published online, the argument can support transparency, not democratization.

If no participation evidence is found, the book should shift from “dashboards make planning more democratic” to “dashboards make planning more visible.”

If the administrative-capacity explanation is stronger, the book should frame dashboards as governance modernization with possible democratic affordances, not as evidence of democratic change.
