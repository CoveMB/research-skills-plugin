## Central question

How do dashboards become politically significant rather than merely technical tools, and what follows from treating them as sites of decision-making, visibility, and power?

## Counterarguments to include

- Dashboards may reflect existing institutional priorities rather than create them.
- Users may ignore, reinterpret, or work around dashboards.
- Political effects may come from metrics, policies, or incentives more than the interface itself.
- Some dashboard choices may be practical constraints rather than deliberate political decisions.

## Opening options

1. Open with a concrete dashboard moment: a decision, alert, ranking, threshold, or meeting where dashboard information changes what people notice or do.
2. Open with the apparent neutrality of dashboards, then introduce the puzzle that technical displays may carry political consequences.
3. Open with a missing or invisible category in a dashboard, using absence as the route into the chapter’s argument.

## Ending / bridge to next chapter

The ending should synthesize what the chapter has established: dashboards organize attention and action through specific design and institutional choices, but the strength of that claim depends on case evidence. The bridge should point to the next chapter’s question, such as who designs these systems, who uses them, who is governed by them, or how dashboard politics changes across cases.
